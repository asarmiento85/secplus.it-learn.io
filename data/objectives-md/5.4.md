# 5.4 — Security Compliance

## What you need to know

Compliance is the process of adhering to laws, regulations, standards, and contractual obligations that govern how an organization protects information. The exam tests your knowledge of major regulations (HIPAA, PCI-DSS, SOX, GDPR, FERPA), the consequences of non-compliance (fines, sanctions, reputational damage), and the mechanisms used to demonstrate compliance (attestation, acknowledgement, monitoring). You also need to understand privacy-specific concepts including data subject rights, data retention, and the distinction between due diligence and due care. GDPR and PCI-DSS fine structures are frequently tested.

---

## Compliance Reporting

| Type | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Internal Reporting** | Reports to management and the board showing compliance status | Security dashboards, compliance scorecards, audit findings |
| **External Reporting** | Submissions to regulators, auditors, or business partners | PCI-DSS Report on Compliance (ROC), HIPAA attestation, SOC 2 report |

- Internal compliance reports help leadership identify gaps and prioritize remediation
- External reports demonstrate compliance to regulators, customers, and partners
- Failure to report timely can itself be a compliance violation (e.g., GDPR 72-hour breach notification)

## Key Regulations

| Regulation | Full Name | Applies To | Key Requirements |
|---|---|---|---|
| **HIPAA** | Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act | Healthcare providers, insurers, business associates | Protect PHI (Protected Health Information); Privacy Rule, Security Rule, Breach Notification Rule |
| **PCI-DSS** | Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard | Any entity that stores, processes, or transmits cardholder data | 12 requirements including encryption, access control, monitoring, vulnerability management |
| **SOX** | Sarbanes-Oxley Act | Publicly traded US companies | Accurate financial reporting; IT controls over financial systems; CEO/CFO attestation |
| **GDPR** | General Data Protection Regulation | Any org processing EU residents' personal data | Lawful basis for processing, data subject rights, 72-hour breach notification, DPO requirement |
| **FERPA** | Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act | Educational institutions receiving federal funding | Protects student education records; parental rights until age 18 |
| **GLBA** | Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act | Financial institutions | Safeguards Rule: protect customer financial data; Privacy Notice requirement |
| **CCPA** | California Consumer Privacy Act | Businesses serving California residents (revenue/data thresholds) | Consumer rights to know, delete, and opt-out of sale of personal data |

### PCI-DSS Deep Dive

PCI-DSS has 12 requirements organized around 6 goals:

| Goal | Requirements |
|---|---|
| Build and maintain a secure network | 1. Install firewalls; 2. Don't use vendor defaults |
| Protect cardholder data | 3. Protect stored data; 4. Encrypt data in transit |
| Vulnerability management | 5. Use antivirus; 6. Develop secure systems |
| Access control | 7. Restrict access by need-to-know; 8. Assign unique IDs; 9. Restrict physical access |
| Monitor and test | 10. Track and monitor all access; 11. Test security systems regularly |
| Information security policy | 12. Maintain an information security policy |

### GDPR Key Facts

- Applies to all organizations worldwide that process **EU residents'** personal data
- **72-hour breach notification** requirement to supervisory authority
- Fines: up to **€20 million or 4% of global annual revenue** (whichever is higher)
- Requires a **Data Protection Officer (DPO)** for some organizations
- Legal bases for processing: consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, legitimate interests
- **Data subjects** have rights: access, rectification, erasure (right to be forgotten), portability, objection, restriction

## Consequences of Non-Compliance

| Consequence | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Fines** | Monetary penalties from regulators | GDPR: up to €20M or 4% global revenue; PCI-DSS: $5,000–$100,000/month |
| **Sanctions** | Operational restrictions imposed by regulators or payment networks | Loss of ability to process credit cards; banned from market |
| **Reputational Damage** | Loss of customer trust and brand value | Public breach disclosure; media coverage; customer churn |
| **Loss of License** | Revocation of license to operate in regulated space | Healthcare license revoked; banking charter suspended |
| **Contractual Impacts** | Breach of contract with clients or partners | Contract termination; liquidated damages; indemnification claims |
| **Criminal Liability** | Criminal prosecution of individuals for willful non-compliance | HIPAA criminal penalties up to 10 years imprisonment |

## Compliance Monitoring

| Concept | Description |
|---|---|
| **Due Diligence** | Proactive research and investigation to understand compliance obligations before acting | Researching applicable laws before entering a new market |
| **Due Care** | Ongoing, reasonable protective measures to maintain compliance | Continuously patching systems, training employees, reviewing controls |
| **Attestation** | Formal declaration — an officer signs a document stating that controls are operating effectively | SOX requires CEO/CFO to attest to financial control accuracy |
| **Acknowledgement** | Employee confirmation that they received, read, and understand a policy | Signed AUP at onboarding; annual policy re-acknowledgement |

**Due diligence vs. Due care:**
- **Due diligence** = knowing what you should do (the research phase)
- **Due care** = actually doing it (the implementation and maintenance phase)
- Both are required to demonstrate reasonable behavior to regulators and courts

---

## Internal vs. External Monitoring

| Type | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Internal Monitoring** | Ongoing self-assessment of compliance status | Automated compliance scans, internal audit team reviews, policy violation tracking |
| **External Monitoring** | Third-party or regulator review of compliance | PCI QSA assessment, HIPAA OCR audit, SOC 2 audit, ISO 27001 certification audit |

## Privacy Compliance

Privacy is a subset of compliance — managing personal data with appropriate protections and respecting individual rights.

| Concept | Description |
|---|---|
| **Legal Implications** | Privacy laws vary by jurisdiction — what's legal in one country may be prohibited in another |
| **Data Subject Rights (GDPR)** | Right to access, rectification, erasure, portability, objection, and restriction of processing |
| **Controller vs. Processor** | Controller determines purpose; Processor executes — both have GDPR obligations |
| **Data Ownership** | Clearly define who owns data collected from customers, employees, partners |
| **Data Inventory** | Know what personal data you hold, where it is, and who has access — required for compliance |
| **Data Retention** | Policies defining how long data is kept and when it must be securely destroyed |
| **Right to Be Forgotten** | GDPR Article 17 — data subjects can request erasure of their personal data |
| **Data Minimization** | Collect only the data necessary for the stated purpose (GDPR principle) |
| **Purpose Limitation** | Data collected for one purpose cannot be reused for an incompatible purpose |

### Privacy Roles (GDPR)

| Role | Obligation |
|---|---|
| **Data Controller** | Ensure lawful basis for processing; fulfill data subject rights; appoint DPO if required; notify of breaches |
| **Data Processor** | Process only on controller's documented instructions; assist with security; notify controller of breaches immediately |
| **Data Protection Officer (DPO)** | Advises on GDPR compliance; monitors compliance; cooperates with supervisory authority |

## Exam Tips

- **PCI-DSS has 12 requirements** — know the 6 goal categories; fines can reach $100,000/month for non-compliance
- **GDPR fines** are the higher of €20 million or **4% of global annual revenue** — a massive financial risk
- **GDPR 72-hour breach notification** to the supervisory authority — not 72 hours to the data subject
- **HIPAA** protects **PHI** (Protected Health Information) — healthcare providers and their business associates must comply
- **SOX** requires CEO and CFO to **attest** to the accuracy of financial controls — willful false attestation is a crime
- **FERPA** protects student education records — parents have rights until the student turns 18, then rights transfer to student
- **Due diligence** = knowing your obligations; **Due care** = actually fulfilling them — both matter in legal defense
- **Attestation** = formal signed declaration by an officer; **Acknowledgement** = employee confirmation of policy receipt
- **Right to be forgotten** is GDPR Article 17 — organizations must be able to honor erasure requests
- **CCPA** applies to California residents — not all businesses; check revenue and data volume thresholds
