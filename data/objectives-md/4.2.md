# 4.2 — Asset Management Security

## What you need to know

Asset management is the disciplined tracking of every hardware, software, and data asset throughout its entire lifecycle — from procurement to disposal. Security+ tests your ability to identify the correct process for each lifecycle phase and, critically, the correct data sanitization method for a given scenario. Disposal is heavily tested: you must know when to overwrite, degauss, or physically destroy media based on its type and sensitivity classification.

---

## Asset Lifecycle Phases

| Phase | Key Activities | Security Considerations |
|---|---|---|
| **Acquisition / Procurement** | Purchase hardware, software, cloud services | Security review before purchase; vendor risk assessment |
| **Assignment / Accounting** | Assign owner; classify asset | Ownership, classification labels, asset tagging |
| **Monitoring / Tracking** | Inventory management; ongoing visibility | Asset enumeration, CMDB, software licensing |
| **Disposal / Decommissioning** | Retire asset securely | Data sanitization, certificate of destruction, data retention |

---

## Acquisition and Procurement

- **Security review before purchase** — evaluate vendor security practices, third-party risk
- Assess whether the vendor has documented security controls (SOC 2 reports, security questionnaires)
- For software: evaluate **open source dependencies**, licensing, and known vulnerabilities
- For cloud services: review the **shared responsibility model** — what does the vendor secure vs. you?
- **Supply chain risk** — malicious hardware/software can be inserted during manufacturing or shipping

---

## Assignment and Accounting

### Asset Ownership
- Every asset must have a **designated owner** (individual or team responsible for security)
- Owner is accountable for classification decisions, access control, and proper disposal
- Owners are not necessarily the users — a database may be owned by the DBA team but used by many

### Asset Classification

| Classification Level | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Public** | No harm if disclosed | Marketing materials, public website content |
| **Internal / Private** | Intended for internal use; limited harm if disclosed | Internal policies, org charts |
| **Confidential / Sensitive** | Could cause significant harm if disclosed | Customer PII, financial records |
| **Restricted / Top Secret** | Severe harm if disclosed | Trade secrets, national security data |

- **Labeling** — physical and digital labels must reflect classification
- **Handling requirements** differ by classification — who can access, how it's stored, how it's transmitted

---

## Monitoring and Tracking

### Asset Inventory
- Maintain a complete **hardware asset inventory** — every device, serial number, location, owner
- Maintain a **software asset inventory** — every application, version, license status
- **Asset enumeration** — actively discovering all assets on the network (network scanning tools)
- Automated discovery tools: Nmap, Nessus, Microsoft Intune, Tanium

### CMDB (Configuration Management Database)
- Centralized repository of all **configuration items (CIs)** and their relationships
- Tracks hardware, software, services, and dependencies
- Feeds into **change management** — understand impact before making changes
- Supports **incident response** — quickly identify affected systems

### Software Licensing
- **License compliance** — audit software installations vs. purchased licenses
- **Shadow IT** — unauthorized software installed without IT knowledge; significant security risk
- Automated tools scan for unlicensed or unauthorized software

## Disposal and Decommissioning

This is the most heavily tested area. You must know the correct sanitization method for each media type and sensitivity level.

### Data Sanitization Methods

| Method | Description | Best For | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Clearing** | Overwriting data with new data (logical sanitization) | Reuse within organization | Not suitable for classified data; some recovery possible |
| **Purging** | More thorough removal using cryptographic erase or advanced overwrite | Reuse outside organization | Depends on media type |
| **Destroying** | Physical destruction — shredding, incineration, disintegration | Classified / highest sensitivity | Cannot reuse media |

### Overwriting (Clearing)
- Writes patterns (zeros, ones, random) over all storage sectors
- **DoD 5220.22-M** standard: 3-pass overwrite
- **Gutmann method**: 35-pass overwrite (considered excessive by modern standards)
- **One pass** of random data is sufficient for most modern hard drives per NIST SP 800-88
- **Does NOT work reliably on SSDs** — due to wear leveling, some sectors may not be overwritten
- For SSDs: use **cryptographic erase** or **ATA Secure Erase** commands

### Degaussing
- Exposes magnetic media to a powerful magnetic field, randomizing magnetic domains
- **Works on**: HDDs, magnetic tape, floppy disks
- **Does NOT work on**: SSDs, USB drives, optical media (CDs/DVDs), non-magnetic media
- After degaussing, the drive is typically **unusable** (servo tracks destroyed on modern HDDs)
- Degaussed media should be physically destroyed afterward if highly sensitive

### Physical Destruction Methods

| Method | Description |
|---|---|
| **Shredding** | Feed media through industrial shredder; reduces to small particles |
| **Incineration** | Burn media at high temperature |
| **Disintegration** | Specialized equipment reduces media to tiny fragments |
| **Pulverizing** | Hammer or crush the media |
| **Drilling/Crushing** | Physically damage drive platters or chips |

### Certificate of Destruction
- **Formal documentation** issued by a third-party vendor confirming media was destroyed
- Required for regulated industries (healthcare, finance, government)
- Should include: description of media, quantity, serial numbers, destruction method, date, witness signature
- Provides legal proof of compliance with data handling regulations (HIPAA, PCI DSS, GDPR)

---

## Data Retention Policies

- Define **how long** data must be retained based on legal, regulatory, and business requirements
- HIPAA requires 6 years; PCI DSS requires 1 year for transaction logs
- After retention period expires, data must be **properly disposed of** using approved sanitization methods
- **Legal hold** — suspends normal deletion policies when data is subject to litigation or investigation
- Automated systems should enforce retention policies — do not rely on manual deletion

---

## Sanitization Method Selection Guide

| Scenario | Recommended Method |
|---|---|
| Drive being reused internally, non-sensitive data | Clearing (overwriting) |
| Drive being donated or sold, general data | Purging (cryptographic erase or thorough overwrite) |
| Drive with classified/highly sensitive data, being retired | Physical destruction (shredding) |
| Magnetic tape with confidential data | Degaussing + physical destruction |
| SSD with sensitive data | Cryptographic erase or physical destruction |
| Drive that failed and cannot boot | Physical destruction |

---

## Exam Tips

- **Degaussing only works on magnetic media** — it does nothing to SSDs, USB drives, or optical discs.
- **Overwriting (clearing) may not fully sanitize SSDs** — wear leveling means some blocks may be skipped.
- **Cryptographic erase** is the preferred SSD sanitization method — destroy the encryption key and all data becomes unreadable.
- **Certificate of destruction** = third-party verification document for destroyed media — required for compliance.
- **CMDB** tracks configuration items and their relationships — distinct from a simple asset inventory.
- **Shadow IT** is unauthorized software — it represents both a security risk and a license compliance problem.
- Know the three-tier sanitization hierarchy: **Clearing < Purging < Destroying** (increasing thoroughness).
- **Legal hold suspends destruction** — even if a retention period has expired, data under legal hold cannot be deleted.
- Asset **ownership** means accountability for security decisions — not necessarily daily use.
- The exam may describe a scenario with classified government data on a HDD — correct answer is **physical destruction**, not degaussing alone.
- **Acquisition security review** is done **before** purchase — proactive, not reactive.
