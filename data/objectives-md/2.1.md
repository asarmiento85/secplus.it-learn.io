# 2.1 — Threat Actors and Motivations

## What you need to know

Threat actors are individuals or groups that pose a risk to an organization's systems and data. The Security+ exam requires you to identify each actor type by their defining characteristics — especially their **level of sophistication**, **available resources**, and **primary motivation**. Understanding *why* an actor attacks is just as important as knowing *who* they are, because motivation shapes the tactics, targets, and persistence of the attack.

---

## Threat Actor Types

### Nation-State Actors

Nation-states are government-sponsored or government-directed hacking groups.

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Sophistication** | Highest — access to zero-days, custom malware, large teams |
| **Resources** | Virtually unlimited (government-funded) |
| **Goals** | Espionage, sabotage, IP theft, disruption of critical infrastructure |
| **Persistence** | Long-term; often lurk undetected for months or years |
| **Key term** | APT — **Advanced Persistent Threat** |
| **Examples** | Lazarus Group (DPRK), APT28/Fancy Bear (Russia), APT41 (China) |

- APT = Advanced **Persistent** Threat — *persistent* is the defining word; they maintain long-term access
- Targets: defense contractors, utilities, financial systems, healthcare, government agencies
- Often use supply chain attacks and zero-day exploits unavailable to others
- Attacks may look like normal traffic for months; goal is stealth, not speed

---

### Unskilled Attacker (Script Kiddie)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Sophistication** | Low — relies entirely on tools written by others |
| **Resources** | Minimal |
| **Goals** | Notoriety, curiosity, bragging rights |
| **Persistence** | Low — moves on if attack doesn't work quickly |

- Runs existing exploit frameworks (Metasploit, automated scanners) without understanding them
- May cause real damage despite low skill — automated tools are powerful
- Not targeted; conducts opportunistic scans and tries known exploits against anything found
- The term "script kiddie" is the official CompTIA label for this actor type

---

### Hacktivist

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Sophistication** | Low to moderate |
| **Resources** | Minimal to moderate (volunteer-based, crowdsourced) |
| **Goals** | Political or ideological message; public embarrassment of target |
| **Common TTPs** | DDoS, website defacement, data leaks, doxxing |
| **Example** | Anonymous, LulzSec |

- Motivation is **philosophical/political** — not financial
- Will leak sensitive data to maximize public embarrassment
- Targets: government agencies, corporations seen as unethical, law enforcement
- Success is measured in attention, not money

---

### Insider Threat

Insiders already have **legitimate access** to systems — making them uniquely dangerous.

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Malicious insider** | Intentionally exfiltrates data, sabotages systems, or assists an external attacker |
| **Unintentional insider** | Accidentally causes harm — clicks phishing link, misconfigures a system |
| **Negligent insider** | Knows the policy but ignores it (e.g., taping password to monitor) |

- Often the **hardest threat to detect** because normal access patterns don't trigger alerts
- Includes disgruntled employees, contractors, and trusted third-party partners
- Common scenario: departing employee exfiltrating IP before their last day
- Mitigation: least privilege, DLP tools, user behavior analytics (UBA), separation of duties
- **Shadow IT** is a related concept — employees deploying unauthorized systems; risky but usually not malicious

---

### Organized Crime

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Sophistication** | Moderate to high — operate like businesses with dev, ops, and support teams |
| **Resources** | Substantial (self-funded through criminal proceeds) |
| **Goals** | Financial gain — ransomware, fraud, credential theft, BEC |
| **Persistence** | Moderate — will invest time if ROI is high |

- Operate like professional organizations with specialized roles
- Offer **Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS)** — affiliates "subscribe" to a ransomware platform
- Examples: REvil, Conti, LockBit
- Often operate from jurisdictions with no extradition treaties

---

### Shadow IT

Shadow IT refers to **unauthorized technology** used within an organization without IT approval.

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Intent** | Usually not malicious — employees trying to be more productive |
| **Risk** | Bypasses security controls, creates unmanaged attack surface |
| **Examples** | Personal Dropbox, unapproved SaaS apps, personal devices, unauthorized cloud VMs |

- Not a "threat actor" in the traditional sense, but creates real vulnerabilities
- IT has no visibility into shadow IT → cannot patch, monitor, or protect it
- Common in organizations with overly restrictive approved-tools lists
- Data stored in unsanctioned services may violate compliance requirements

## Threat Actor Attributes

These attributes help classify and compare threat actors across multiple dimensions.

| Attribute | What it Measures | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Internal vs. External** | Is the actor inside or outside the org? | Insiders bypass perimeter controls; externals must first gain access |
| **Resources/Funding** | How much money and personnel does the actor have? | Determines sophistication and duration of attack |
| **Sophistication/Capability** | What tools and techniques can they use? | Low skill = commodity tools; high skill = custom zero-days and novel TTPs |

## Motivations Table

| Motivation | Associated Actor(s) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Data exfiltration** | Nation-state, organized crime, insiders | Stealing data for sale or strategic use |
| **Espionage** | Nation-state (primary), insider (secondary) | Long-term intelligence gathering |
| **Service disruption** | Hacktivist, nation-state, organized crime | DDoS or destructive wiper malware |
| **Blackmail / extortion** | Organized crime | Ransomware, sextortion, DDoS-for-ransom |
| **Financial gain** | Organized crime, some insiders | Primary driver for criminal groups |
| **Philosophical / political** | Hacktivist | Attacks tied to causes or protests |
| **Ethical (whistleblowing)** | Insider | Believes exposure serves the public good |
| **Revenge** | Disgruntled insider | Retaliation for perceived workplace injustice |
| **Disruption / chaos** | Script kiddie, hacktivist | No specific goal beyond damage or attention |
| **Cyberwarfare** | Nation-state | Attacks framed as acts of war against another nation |

## Exam Tips

- **APT = nation-state** — if a question mentions long-term undetected access, government-sponsored attacks, or months-long campaigns, the answer is nation-state/APT
- **Insider threat = hardest to detect** — they already have authorized access; exam loves asking why they're the most dangerous
- **Script kiddies** use tools they don't understand — not sophisticated, but automated tools can still cause damage
- **Organized crime** = financially motivated; think ransomware-as-a-service, BEC, credential theft
- **Hacktivist** = ideologically/politically motivated; think DDoS, defacement, data leaks — not money-driven
- **Shadow IT** is not a malicious actor but creates unmanaged vulnerabilities that other actors can exploit
- Know the difference between **internal vs. external** actors and how each bypasses or is blocked by different controls
- **Resources and sophistication** go hand-in-hand — nation-states have both; script kiddies have neither
- Motivations map directly to actors — the exam may give you a motivation and ask you to identify the actor type
- **Disgruntled employee** exfiltrating data on their last day = classic insider threat scenario; always appears on exams
