# 5.6 — Security Awareness Practices

## What you need to know

Security awareness programs reduce the human element of risk by educating employees to recognize threats and respond appropriately. The exam tests your knowledge of phishing simulation programs, the types of user behaviors that represent security risks, the content of security awareness training, and how programs are measured and maintained. You need to know the different categories of risky behavior (intentional vs. unintentional), the topics that should be covered in training, and how phishing simulations drive training effectiveness. Expect scenario questions asking what type of training addresses a specific employee behavior or threat.

---

## Phishing Simulations

Phishing simulations test employee susceptibility to social engineering by sending controlled fake phishing emails.

| Component | Description |
|---|---|
| **Campaign** | Planned series of simulated phishing emails sent to employees |
| **Click Rate** | Percentage of employees who click the malicious link — key effectiveness metric |
| **Training Trigger** | Employees who click immediately receive targeted awareness training (teachable moment) |
| **Reporting Mechanism** | Employees can report suspicious emails via a "Report Phishing" button or abuse mailbox |
| **Baseline Assessment** | Initial campaign before training to measure starting click rate |
| **Trend Analysis** | Track improvement in click rates over time across training cycles |

### Phishing Indicators to Recognize

| Indicator | Description |
|---|---|
| **Suspicious Sender** | Display name doesn't match email domain; look-alike domain (paypa1.com vs paypal.com) |
| **Urgency/Pressure** | "Your account will be closed in 24 hours" — creates panic to bypass critical thinking |
| **Mismatched URLs** | Hover shows different destination than display text; URL shorteners hiding real destination |
| **Poor Grammar/Spelling** | Grammatical errors or odd phrasing may indicate phishing (though AI is reducing this indicator) |
| **Unexpected Attachments** | Unsolicited attachments, especially .exe, .zip, .docm (macro-enabled) |
| **Requests for Credentials** | Legitimate organizations rarely ask for passwords via email |
| **Generic Greetings** | "Dear Customer" instead of your name may indicate bulk phishing |

## Anomalous Behavior Recognition

Security awareness training should teach employees to recognize suspicious behavior — in themselves and others.

### Categories of Risky Behavior

| Category | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Risky Behavior** | Actions that knowingly or unknowingly create security risk | Connecting to public WiFi without VPN; plugging in unknown USB drives; working around security controls |
| **Unexpected Behavior** | Unusual activity that may indicate compromise or malicious intent | Accessing systems at 3 AM; bulk downloading data; accessing unrelated departments' files |
| **Unintentional Behavior** | Mistakes made without malicious intent | Sending email to wrong recipient; disposing of documents without shredding; misconfiguring a shared folder |

### Insider Threat Indicators

Employees should be trained to recognize potential insider threat warning signs:

- Attempting to access resources outside of job scope
- Expressing grievances about the organization or management
- Taking work home on unauthorized media
- Attempting to bypass security controls
- Downloading excessive amounts of data before a resignation
- Accessing systems after termination notice

## User Guidance and Training Topics

A comprehensive security awareness program covers a wide range of topics.

| Topic | Key Points to Teach |
|---|---|
| **Policy and Handbooks** | Employees must read, understand, and acknowledge the AUP and security policies; annual re-acknowledgement |
| **Situational Awareness** | Recognize social engineering attempts; maintain physical security (don't let strangers tailgate through doors) |
| **Insider Threat** | Warning signs in colleagues; how to report concerns anonymously; consequences of insider threats |
| **Password Management** | Use a password manager; enable MFA everywhere; don't reuse passwords; don't share credentials |
| **Removable Media** | Never plug in unknown USB drives (USB drop attacks); use only approved, encrypted media |
| **Social Engineering** | Recognize pretexting, vishing (phone-based), impersonation, quid pro quo attacks |
| **Operational Security (OPSEC)** | Don't discuss sensitive work on social media; be careful what you post publicly; shoulder surfing risk |
| **Hybrid/Remote Work** | Always use VPN on home/public networks; lock screen when stepping away; prevent shoulder surfing; secure home router |
| **Removable Cables** | Be cautious of O.MG cables and juice jacking (charging via USB in public) — use a USB data blocker or AC charger |

### Social Engineering Attack Types

| Attack | Description |
|---|---|
| **Pretexting** | Creating a fabricated scenario to manipulate a victim (e.g., "I'm from IT, I need your password") |
| **Vishing** | Voice phishing — social engineering via phone call |
| **Smishing** | SMS phishing — malicious links sent via text message |
| **Spear Phishing** | Targeted phishing aimed at a specific individual or organization |
| **Whaling** | Spear phishing targeting executives (high-value targets) |
| **Impersonation** | Posing as a trusted person or authority figure |
| **Quid Pro Quo** | Offering something (fake tech support) in exchange for credentials |
| **Tailgating/Piggybacking** | Following an authorized person through a secured door without badging in |

## Training Delivery and Program Development

### Training Program Lifecycle

| Phase | Description |
|---|---|
| **Needs Assessment** | Identify what behaviors and risks the program must address; analyze incident data, phishing results |
| **Audience Analysis** | Tailor content for different roles (executives vs. IT staff vs. general employees) |
| **Content Development** | Create engaging, role-specific content; use real-world scenarios |
| **Delivery Method Selection** | LMS, video, live training, simulations, posters, newsletters |
| **Execution** | Assign training in LMS; track completion rates; send reminders |
| **Measurement** | Track phishing click rates, quiz scores, incident report rates, policy violations |
| **Review and Improvement** | Analyze metrics; update content based on new threats; refresh annually |

### Training Frequency

| Type | Frequency |
|---|---|
| **Initial/Onboarding Training** | Before or immediately upon starting employment; before systems access |
| **Annual Training** | Minimum yearly requirement for most compliance frameworks |
| **Quarterly Training** | Recommended for high-risk roles or organizations with elevated threat levels |
| **Event-Triggered Training** | After a security incident, policy violation, or clicking a phishing simulation |
| **Role-Specific Training** | Additional training for privileged users, IT staff, executives, and HR |

## Measuring Program Effectiveness

| Metric | What It Measures |
|---|---|
| **Phishing Click Rate** | Percentage of employees who clicked a simulated phishing link — lower is better |
| **Phishing Report Rate** | Percentage who correctly reported the simulation — higher is better |
| **Training Completion Rate** | Percentage who completed assigned training modules |
| **Quiz/Assessment Scores** | Knowledge retention after training |
| **Repeat Offender Rate** | Employees who click multiple phishing simulations — targeted for additional coaching |
| **Incident Reporting Rate** | How often employees report suspicious activity — increase indicates improved culture |
| **Policy Violation Rate** | Number of detected policy violations over time |

## Reporting and Monitoring Mechanisms

- **Abuse mailbox** (security@company.com or phish@company.com): dedicated email address for reporting suspected phishing
- **Report Phishing button**: Add-in to email client (Outlook, Gmail) that sends the email to the security team with full headers
- **Anonymous reporting hotline**: Employees can report suspicious colleague behavior without fear of retaliation
- **SIEM integration**: Phishing reports and simulated click data fed into SIEM for trend analysis

## Exam Tips

- **Phishing simulations** create "teachable moments" — employees who click receive immediate training, not punishment
- **Risky behavior** is acting unsafely (USB drop); **Unexpected behavior** is unusual access patterns; **Unintentional** is accidental mistakes
- The **Report Phishing button** and **abuse mailbox** are the two mechanisms for employees to report suspicious emails
- **Vishing** = voice phishing (phone); **Smishing** = SMS phishing; **Whaling** = spear phishing targeting executives
- **OPSEC** training teaches employees not to share sensitive information publicly — social media hygiene is a key topic
- **Tailgating** (piggybacking) is a physical social engineering technique — should be covered in situational awareness training
- **USB drop attacks** rely on human curiosity — employees should never plug in found USB drives
- **Annual training** is the minimum standard; quarterly is recommended for higher-risk environments
- **Click rate trending downward** over time is the key metric that proves a phishing simulation program is working
- **Hybrid/remote work** training should include VPN use, shoulder surfing prevention, and home network security
