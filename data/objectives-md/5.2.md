# 5.2 — Risk Management

## What you need to know

Risk management is the process of identifying, analyzing, and responding to risks that could affect an organization's information assets. The exam heavily tests the quantitative risk formulas (AV, EF, SLE, ARO, ALE), the four risk treatment strategies (transfer, accept, avoid, mitigate), and the Business Impact Analysis metrics (RTO, RPO, MTTR, MTBF). You also need to distinguish between qualitative and quantitative analysis, understand risk appetite vs. risk tolerance, and know when to use each risk treatment strategy. Calculation questions with ALE appear regularly — memorize the formulas.

---

## Risk Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Asset** | Anything of value that needs protection (data, systems, people, processes) |
| **Threat** | Potential cause of an unwanted event (hacker, natural disaster, insider) |
| **Vulnerability** | Weakness that a threat can exploit |
| **Risk** | Likelihood that a threat will exploit a vulnerability and cause harm |
| **Likelihood/Probability** | Chance that a risk event will occur |
| **Impact** | Business harm if the risk event occurs |
| **Inherent Risk** | Risk before any controls are applied |
| **Residual Risk** | Risk remaining after controls are applied |
| **Control Risk** | Risk that a control will fail |

- **Risk = Likelihood × Impact** (conceptual formula; not a strict calculation for qualitative)
- **Residual risk** always exists — you cannot eliminate all risk

---

## Risk Assessment Types

| Type | Description | When Used |
|---|---|---|
| **Ad hoc** | Unscheduled; triggered by a specific event or concern | After a breach, new threat intelligence, major system change |
| **Recurring** | Periodic assessment on a set schedule | Annual risk assessment required by policy |
| **One-time** | Performed for a specific project or purpose | Pre-merger due diligence, new system deployment |
| **Continuous** | Ongoing monitoring with real-time risk data | Automated SIEM/vulnerability scanner feeds |

## Risk Analysis Methods

### Qualitative Analysis

- Uses **subjective descriptors**: High, Medium, Low (or 1–5 scales)
- Faster and requires **less data** — useful when numerical data is unavailable
- Results in a **risk matrix** or **heat map**
- Relies on expert judgment, not mathematical precision

### Quantitative Analysis

- Uses **actual dollar values and statistical data**
- More objective and defensible to management and auditors
- Requires more data and time to complete
- Produces results management can compare directly to control costs

## Quantitative Risk Formulas

These formulas are almost guaranteed to appear on the exam. Memorize them all.

| Term | Formula / Definition |
|---|---|
| **Asset Value (AV)** | The monetary value of the asset |
| **Exposure Factor (EF)** | Percentage of asset value lost in a single incident (0–100%) |
| **Single Loss Expectancy (SLE)** | AV × EF |
| **Annualized Rate of Occurrence (ARO)** | How many times the incident is expected to occur per year |
| **Annualized Loss Expectancy (ALE)** | SLE × ARO |
| **Value of a Control** | ALE (before control) − ALE (after control) − Annual Cost of Control |

**Example calculation:**
- Server AV = $100,000
- Fire destroys 40% of its value → EF = 0.40
- SLE = $100,000 × 0.40 = **$40,000**
- Fire expected once every 10 years → ARO = 0.1
- ALE = $40,000 × 0.1 = **$4,000/year**
- Sprinkler system costs $500/year and reduces fire risk by 90%
- New ALE = $4,000 × 0.1 = $400
- Value of control = $4,000 − $400 − $500 = **$3,100** (worth installing)

## Risk Register

A risk register is a living document that tracks all identified risks.

| Component | Description |
|---|---|
| **Risk Description** | What is the risk and what asset/process does it affect |
| **Likelihood** | Probability of occurrence (qualitative or quantitative) |
| **Impact** | Business impact if the risk occurs |
| **Risk Score** | Likelihood × Impact rating |
| **Risk Owner** | Person accountable for managing the risk |
| **Risk Response** | Treatment strategy chosen (avoid, mitigate, transfer, accept) |
| **Current Controls** | Controls already in place |
| **Residual Risk** | Risk remaining after controls |
| **Key Risk Indicators (KRIs)** | Metrics that signal when risk is increasing |
| **Risk Threshold** | The level at which action must be taken |

## Risk Appetite vs. Risk Tolerance

| Concept | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Risk Appetite** | The overall amount and type of risk the organization is willing to pursue or accept |
| **Risk Tolerance** | The acceptable variance around risk appetite; how much deviation is acceptable |
| **Risk Threshold** | The specific level at which the risk becomes unacceptable and must be escalated |

### Risk Appetite Types

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Expansionary** | Willing to accept higher risk for greater reward/growth | Startup accepting technology risk to move fast |
| **Conservative** | Minimizes risk, even at the cost of growth opportunities | Government agency, highly regulated industry |
| **Neutral** | Balanced approach between risk-taking and risk-avoidance | Mid-size enterprise balancing innovation and stability |

## Risk Treatment Strategies

| Strategy | Description | Example | Key Fact |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mitigate** | Implement controls to reduce likelihood or impact | Install firewalls, patch systems, train users | Most common strategy; reduces but doesn't eliminate |
| **Transfer** | Shift the financial impact to a third party | Purchase cyber insurance; outsource to MSSP | Risk itself remains; financial burden shifts |
| **Accept** | Acknowledge risk and take no action | Accept the risk of a printer being offline | Used when cost of mitigation exceeds impact |
| **Avoid** | Stop the activity that creates the risk | Shut down a vulnerable service; exit a risky market | Eliminates the risk but may impact business |

### Acceptance Variants

- **Exemption**: Formal, documented exception to a security policy for a specific system or user — requires management approval
- **Exception**: Temporary deviation from a standard or policy; time-limited, requires justification

## Risk Reporting

- **Risk register** is reported to management and the board regularly
- **Risk dashboard** provides at-a-glance view of top risks and KRI trends
- Board expects **business-language** reporting — dollar impact, not just technical details
- **Escalation path**: Risk owner → CISO → C-suite → Board of Directors

---

## Business Impact Analysis (BIA)

BIA identifies the most critical business functions and defines recovery requirements.

| Metric | Full Name | Definition | Key Point |
|---|---|---|---|
| **RTO** | Recovery Time Objective | Maximum acceptable time a system can be offline | If RTO = 4 hours, system must be restored within 4 hours |
| **RPO** | Recovery Point Objective | Maximum acceptable data loss measured in time | If RPO = 1 hour, backups must occur at least every hour |
| **MTTR** | Mean Time To Repair | Average time to repair a failed component and restore service | Measures IT efficiency in recovery |
| **MTBF** | Mean Time Between Failures | Average time between system failures (reliability metric) | Higher MTBF = more reliable system |
| **MTTF** | Mean Time To Failure | Average time before a non-repairable component fails | Used for hardware like hard drives |

**Key relationship:**
- **RTO** drives your DR strategy — it tells you how fast you need to recover
- **RPO** drives your backup frequency — it tells you how much data loss is acceptable
- Shorter RTO and RPO = more expensive recovery solutions (hot site, synchronous replication)

## Exam Tips

- **SLE = AV × EF** and **ALE = SLE × ARO** — memorize both; calculation questions appear frequently
- **Value of a control** = ALE before − ALE after − cost of control; a positive value means the control is worth implementing
- **Risk transfer** shifts financial impact (insurance) — the risk event can still happen; it's not eliminated
- **Risk avoidance** completely eliminates the risk by stopping the activity — but may have business impact
- **Exemption** = formal documented exception to policy; **Exception** = temporary deviation
- **Qualitative** = fast, subjective (High/Med/Low); **Quantitative** = precise, uses dollar values
- **RTO** = how quickly you must recover (downtime limit); **RPO** = how much data you can afford to lose (backup frequency)
- **MTBF** measures reliability between failures; **MTTR** measures how fast you can fix it
- **Risk appetite** is set by the board; **risk tolerance** is the acceptable variance around that appetite
- **ARO less than 1** means the event occurs less than once per year — e.g., ARO = 0.25 means once every 4 years
