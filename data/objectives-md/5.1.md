# 5.1 — Elements of Effective Security Governance

## What you need to know

Security governance is the framework of policies, standards, procedures, and guidelines that an organization uses to manage and direct its security posture. The exam tests your ability to distinguish between the different tiers of governance documents — especially the difference between guidelines (optional), policies (mandatory), standards (specific requirements), and procedures (step-by-step instructions). You also need to know the roles involved in data governance (owner, custodian, controller, processor, subject) and the various external drivers that shape governance programs. Expect scenario questions asking which document type applies to a given situation, and questions about GDPR-specific roles.

---

## Governance Document Hierarchy

Understanding the hierarchy is critical — each layer flows from the one above it.

| Tier | Type | Mandatory? | Purpose | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Policy** | Yes | High-level mandatory statement of intent | "All data must be encrypted at rest" |
| 2 | **Standard** | Yes | Specific, measurable requirements that implement policy | "AES-256 required for all stored data" |
| 3 | **Procedure** | Yes | Step-by-step instructions for performing tasks | "Run BitLocker wizard, record key in vault" |
| 4 | **Guideline** | No | Recommendations and best practices | "Consider using a password manager" |

- **Policy** = the "what" and "why" — mandatory, approved by senior management
- **Standard** = the "how much" and "how many" — defines specific metrics
- **Procedure** = the "how" step by step — operational instructions
- **Guideline** = the "should" not "shall" — advisory only, no enforcement

---

## Policies

Policies are mandatory high-level management statements. Every other governance document traces back to a policy.

| Policy | Purpose | Key Elements |
|---|---|---|
| **Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)** | Defines what employees can and cannot do with company assets | Permitted uses, prohibited uses, monitoring notice, disciplinary action |
| **Information Security Policy** | Overall security requirements for the organization | CIA triad commitments, scope, compliance requirements |
| **Business Continuity Policy** | Ensures critical functions continue during disruption | RTO/RPO targets, continuity team roles, activation criteria |
| **Disaster Recovery Policy** | IT systems recovery after a disaster | Recovery priorities, backup requirements, testing frequency |
| **Incident Response Policy** | How to detect, respond to, and recover from incidents | Reporting chain, severity levels, legal/regulatory notification |
| **SDLC Policy** | Security integrated into software development lifecycle | Code review requirements, security testing gates, release approval |
| **Change Management Policy** | Controlling changes to IT systems | CAB approval, testing requirements, rollback plans, documentation |

- The **AUP** governs employee use of company resources — internet, email, devices, and cloud services
- Employees typically sign the AUP during onboarding; re-acknowledgement is often required annually
- **SDLC policy** ensures security is considered during design and development, not just at deployment

---

## Standards

Standards translate policy requirements into specific, measurable technical requirements.

| Standard | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Password Standard** | Minimum 12 characters, upper/lower/number/symbol, 90-day expiry, no reuse of last 10 |
| **Access Control Standard** | Role-based access, least privilege enforcement, quarterly access reviews |
| **Physical Security Standard** | Mantrap required for data centers, badge readers at all entry points, CCTV retention 90 days |
| **Encryption Standard** | AES-256 for data at rest, TLS 1.3 for data in transit, RSA-2048 minimum for key exchange |

- Standards are **mandatory** and **specific** — they give IT teams exact requirements to implement
- Standards reference external frameworks: NIST SP 800-63 for passwords, PCI-DSS for payment card environments
- Updated more frequently than policies as technology changes

---

## Procedures

Procedures provide step-by-step "how-to" instructions for specific tasks.

| Procedure | Description |
|---|---|
| **Change Management Procedure** | Request → impact analysis → CAB review → approval → implementation → validation → documentation |
| **Onboarding Checklist** | Create accounts, assign equipment, deliver AUP for signature, complete security training, assign role-based access |
| **Offboarding Checklist** | Disable accounts, revoke access, recover equipment, preserve email/data per retention policy |
| **Playbook** | Specific response steps for a defined scenario (ransomware, phishing, DLP alert, account compromise) |

- **Playbooks** are procedures specific to incident response — they tell responders exactly what to do for a given scenario
- Procedures ensure consistency, reduce human error, and provide documentation for audits

---

## Guidelines

- **Guidelines** are recommendations, not requirements
- They provide flexibility — "you should use a password manager" vs. "you must use a 12-character password"
- Used when a one-size-fits-all mandatory rule would be too restrictive
- Often guide implementation decisions where multiple valid approaches exist
- Cannot be used to enforce accountability — violations cannot be disciplined

---

## External Considerations

Organizations must account for external drivers that shape governance requirements.

| Category | Examples | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Regulatory** | HIPAA, PCI-DSS, SOX, FERPA, GLBA | Legal obligation to meet specific security requirements or face fines |
| **Legal** | Contracts, NDAs, liability, intellectual property | Contractual obligations, civil liability exposure |
| **Industry Standards** | ISO 27001, NIST CSF, CIS Controls | Demonstrate due care; often required by business partners |
| **Local/Regional** | State breach notification laws, CCPA | Must comply with jurisdiction where customers or data reside |
| **National** | FISMA (US federal agencies), HIPAA, SOX | Federal law requirements applied to specific sectors |
| **Global** | GDPR (EU), cross-border data transfer rules | Applies to any organization worldwide handling EU personal data |

- **Regulatory** requirements are law — non-compliance results in fines, sanctions, or operational shutdown
- **GDPR** applies globally to any organization that processes EU residents' data
- **NIST** and **ISO 27001** are voluntary frameworks but are increasingly expected by regulators and partners

## Governance Structures

| Structure | Description | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Centralized** | Single authority makes all security decisions | Consistent policy, unified control | Slower, less flexibility for business units |
| **Decentralized** | Business units control their own security programs | Responsive to local needs | Inconsistency, coverage gaps |
| **Board of Directors** | Highest oversight body; sets risk appetite and tone | Ultimate accountability | High-level only; may lack technical depth |
| **Security Steering Committee** | Cross-functional group guiding security strategy | Business + security alignment | Requires ongoing coordination |
| **Change Advisory Board (CAB)** | Reviews and approves proposed changes to IT systems | Reduces change-related risk | Can slow deployment velocity |
| **Government Entities** | Regulatory bodies — OCC, FTC, HHS OCR, SEC | External enforcement of compliance | Limited customization; external timeline |

## Roles and Responsibilities

Data governance roles are heavily tested — especially the GDPR-specific roles.

| Role | Description | Who Typically Holds This Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Data Owner** | Accountable for data; decides classification and access controls | Senior management / business unit leader |
| **Data Controller** | Determines the purpose and means of processing personal data (GDPR term) | The organization collecting/using the data |
| **Data Processor** | Processes personal data on behalf of the controller (GDPR term) | Cloud providers, payroll processors, marketing platforms |
| **Data Custodian / Steward** | Implements the controls defined by the data owner; day-to-day data care | IT staff, database administrators |
| **Data Subject** | The individual whose personal data is being processed (GDPR term) | Customer, employee, patient |

**Key distinctions to memorize:**
- **Owner vs. Custodian**: Owner decides policy and is accountable; Custodian implements controls and is responsible
- **Controller vs. Processor**: Controller decides why data is processed; Processor executes the processing tasks
- **Data Subject rights under GDPR**: right to access, erasure (right to be forgotten), portability, and objection

## Exam Tips

- **Guidelines are the only non-mandatory governance tier** — policies, standards, and procedures are all required
- The **AUP** is the policy that governs employee use of company assets — know it covers internet, email, devices, and cloud tools
- **Playbooks** are a type of procedure — step-by-step response instructions for a specific incident type
- **Data Owner** is a senior business leader (accountable), not IT — the **Data Custodian** is the IT person who implements controls
- **Data Controller** determines the "why" of processing; **Data Processor** just executes — both are GDPR terms
- **Data Subject** is the individual person — GDPR grants them rights: access, deletion, portability, objection
- **Centralized governance** = consistent but slower; **Decentralized** = flexible but risk of inconsistency
- The **CAB** (Change Advisory Board) reviews and approves changes — a governance structure for change management
- Regulatory requirements override internal policy — if HIPAA and your policy conflict, HIPAA wins
- GDPR applies to **any organization worldwide** that processes EU residents' personal data, not just EU companies
